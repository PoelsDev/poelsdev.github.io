# Title: The Goofy Knight and the Silly Princess

# Chapter 2: In which the Knight arrives at the castle, the Princess tries not to giggle, and the tablecloth receives an uninvited portion of the main course

There are many things a Knight trains for. Meeting the parents of the woman he loves, it turns out, is not one of them.

"Come for dinner," she had said, easily, the way you might suggest stopping for a pastry on the way home. "My parents would love to meet you."

The Knight had said yes. Of course he had said yes. He was brave. He had faced dragons. He had navigated swamps. He had stared down a very stubborn goat on a toll bridge and not flinched once.

He spent the entire week before dinner in a state of quiet, dignified panic.

It was not, he told himself firmly, a big deal. They were just her parents. Ordinary people. The fact that her father happened to be the King of Antwerpshire was entirely beside the point. The fact that her mother was the Queen was similarly irrelevant. He was a Knight. He dined with important people all the time.

He had never actually dined with important people.

On the evening itself, he arrived at the castle gates precisely on time, having changed his outfit twice and then changed back to the first one, and stood for a moment looking up at the towers and the flags and the general enormity of the whole enterprise. It was a magnificent castle. Grand, beautiful, warm light spilling from tall windows into the evening air.

He straightened his armour, cleared his throat, and knocked.

The Princess opened the door herself, which he had not expected, and she was smiling in a way that was entirely warm and also, he noticed, contained just the faintest flicker of anticipation. As if she was already looking forward to something.

"You look terrified," she said cheerfully.

"I look composed," said the Knight.

"You're gripping your helmet so hard your knuckles have gone white."

He looked down. They had. He loosened his grip.

"Come in," she said, and he could hear her trying not to laugh, and he followed her inside.

The King and Queen were in the great hall, and this was the moment the Knight had been mentally preparing for all week — the formal assessment, the weighing up, the careful parental scrutiny he had been bracing himself against.

The King looked up from his chair and said, "Ah, you must be the Knight. Drink?"

The Queen smiled and said, "Sit down, sit down, dinner's nearly ready."

And that was that.

There was no scrutiny. No formal assessment. No weighing up. They were just — people. Warm, normal, completely unbothered people who happened to live in a very large castle and seemed entirely uninterested in making him feel anything other than welcome.

This, somehow, did not make the Knight less nervous. If anything, it made him more nervous, in the specific way that happens when you have prepared extensively for a storm and the weather turns out to be perfectly pleasant and you have no idea what to do with all that prepared nervousness.

He directed it, mostly, at the dinner table.

The table was long and beautifully set, with a white tablecloth so pristine it seemed to be daring someone to ruin it. The Knight sat down, placed his hands carefully in his lap, and resolved to be perfectly normal.

The King talked about the kingdom. The Queen asked pleasant questions. The Princess sat across from him, composed and attentive, though she had adopted a particular expression the Knight was beginning to recognise — chin slightly down, eyes slightly bright, the expression of someone sitting on a laugh and sitting on it very firmly indeed.

He was doing well. He was being perfectly normal. The conversation flowed, the wine was poured, and the main course arrived — a magnificent roast, fit for, well, a king.

"So," said the King pleasantly, "how did you two first meet?"

It was a simple question. A friendly question. The Knight had the answer ready — he had thought about this, he knew this story, he had lived this story — and he opened his mouth to tell it while simultaneously lifting his fork toward his mouth and misjudging the distance between the two by quite a significant margin.

The fork arrived. His mouth did not.

A portion of the main course, caught in the middle of this miscommunication, made a dignified descent onto the white tablecloth.

There was a brief silence.

"We met—" said the Knight, with tremendous composure, as if nothing had happened. He did not look down. He looked directly at the King with the focused expression of a man who has decided that if he does not acknowledge something, it will cease to exist. He told the story carefully, calmly, and at no point mentioned the fork.

The King nodded warmly. "Wonderful."

The Queen reached over and placed a napkin gently over the affected area of the tablecloth, with the serene efficiency of someone who had seen far stranger things and was not going to make a fuss about any of them. She said nothing. She simply smiled and refilled his glass.

The Knight finally allowed himself to glance at the Princess.

She was staring very intently at a spot somewhere above his left shoulder. Her jaw was tight. Her eyes were watering very slightly. She was, by any reasonable measure, about thirty seconds from completely losing the battle she was currently fighting with herself.

He looked away quickly.

Dinner continued. The King and Queen asked more questions, laughed easily, told stories of their own, and treated the Knight throughout with a warmth so genuine and uncomplicated that by the time dessert arrived he had almost — almost — relaxed.

It was only later, walking back through the castle courtyard toward the gate, that the Princess finally let it out — a laugh she had clearly been saving for some time, bright and helpless and completely delighted.

"You missed your own mouth," she said.

"The fork was at an unusual angle," said the Knight.

"It was a completely normal angle."

"I was gesturing."

"You weren't gesturing. You were just nervous."

He wanted to argue. He considered arguing. He looked at her laughing in the warm light of the courtyard torches and found that he didn't want to argue at all, actually. He wanted to remember this — her laughing, the castle behind her, the night air, the way this felt.

"They liked you, you know," she said, once she'd recovered. "They really did."

"I spilled food on their tablecloth."

"They didn't care even slightly."

He thought about the King's easy questions, the Queen's quiet napkin, the way neither of them had made him feel anything other than welcome from the very first moment.

"No," he said. "I suppose they didn't."

The Princess smiled at him — not the almost-laughing smile from across the dinner table, but a softer one, the kind that meant something. He felt it somewhere in the vicinity of the same place he'd felt it in the theatre, that first night.

He walked her back to the castle door, said goodnight, and made his way home through the lamplit streets of Antwerpshire feeling, despite everything — despite the nerves and the fork and the tablecloth — like the evening had gone rather well.

Behind him, through the tall castle windows, he could hear laughter.

He smiled all the way home.
