# Title: The Goofy Knight and the Silly Princess

# Chapter 1: In which a knight loses his trousers (almost)

Once upon a time, in the bustling kingdom of Antwerpshire, there lived a Knight who was — by most accounts — quite brave. He had slain a dragon or two, navigated treacherous swamps, and once, memorably, talked his way out of a toll bridge standoff with a very stubborn goat. Brave, yes. Composed? That was another matter entirely.

On one particular Tuesday morning, the Knight had a problem. His favourite pair of armour-plated trousers — the ones with the comfortable knee guards and the slightly dented left buckle — had finally given up the ghost. Rusted through at the seams, the squire had said, with the solemn tone of someone announcing a funeral.

Simple enough, one might think. Antwerpshire was a fine kingdom with no shortage of shops. He would simply pop in somewhere, find a matching pair, and be done with it.

He was not done with it.

The first shop — Ye Olde Steel & Buckle on the high street — had plenty of trousers, none of which matched his breastplate even slightly. Too bronze, too gold, too aggressively decorative. One pair had little engraved roses on the shins. He put those back very quickly.

The second shop smelled strongly of old leather and disappointment. A cheerful merchant presented him with seventeen options. The Knight tried on four of them, got stuck in one, and left with nothing.

It was in the third shop — a cluttered, wonderfully chaotic little emporium called The Rusty Relic — that something unexpected happened.

He was mid-struggle with a particularly stubborn pair of trousers, one leg on and one leg most certainly not, hopping in a slow desperate circle, when a voice came from somewhere behind a rack of secondhand gauntlets.

"Do you… need a hand?"

The Knight went very still. Then, very slowly, he turned his head. His visor, which he had not remembered to lift, made the motion significantly more dramatic than he intended.

It was a Princess. She was browsing — or had been browsing — and was now watching him with the expression of someone trying very hard to keep a straight face and not quite managing it.

"No," said the Knight, with great dignity.

A pause.

"…Yes," he admitted.

The Princess laughed — not unkindly, but freely, the way you laugh when something is genuinely funny and there's no point pretending otherwise. She crossed the shop floor, crouched down, and with the calm efficiency of someone who had untangled far more complicated problems than a stuck knee guard, she freed the buckle in about four seconds.

"There," she said, standing back up.

"Thank you," said the Knight. He straightened up. Cleared his throat. Tried to look like someone who had the situation completely under control all along. "I was just… testing the latch mechanism."

"Of course," said the Princess, and smiled in a way that suggested she believed absolutely none of that.

She asked what he was looking for. He explained, perhaps at slightly more length than necessary, about the breastplate and the engravings and the very specific shade of silver that nothing in this entire kingdom seemed able to match. She listened with the patient attention of someone who found this genuinely interesting rather than deeply tedious, which he appreciated enormously.

"I know a few other shops," she said. "If you'd like."

He would like.

And so they went. The fourth shop had a promising selection that turned out, on closer inspection, to be entirely the wrong shade. The fifth shop was staffed by a merchant who kept insisting that bronze and silver were essentially the same colour, an argument the Knight found personally offensive. The sixth shop was closed — they stood outside it for a moment in companionable silence, appreciating the absurdity together.

By the seventh shop, they had stopped talking about trousers almost entirely.

They talked about everything else instead — the kind of everything that tumbles out easily when you are walking through cobbled streets with someone whose company feels, unexpectedly and immediately, like somewhere you want to stay. She was funny, the Princess. Properly funny, not just politely funny. She had opinions about things and expressed them without apology. She laughed at her own jokes before she'd finished telling them, which the Knight found completely charming and would never, under any circumstances, admit.

The seventh shop, as it happened, had the trousers. A perfect match — silver, clean lines, sensible knee guards, not a rose engraving in sight. The Knight held them up. The Princess nodded with the gravity of a royal decree. He bought them without even checking the price, which was not something he normally did, and did not examine too closely why.

They stepped back out into the afternoon streets of Antwerpshire, the trouser quest officially concluded, and the Knight became aware of a mildly alarming sensation: he did not want the day to end.

As it turned out, he didn't have to.

"There's a play tonight," said the Princess, almost as an afterthought, as they stood at a crossroads in the last of the golden afternoon light. "At the theatre on Mercator Square. It's about a group of men who decide to go through life permanently, mildly drunk." She paused. "I know that sounds strange. It's very good."

The Knight considered this for exactly as long as it took to think yes, obviously, immediately.

"I'll come," he said, in a tone he hoped suggested he was simply being agreeable, and not that his heart had done something slightly alarming just now.

It had, though.

Sitting in the warm dark of the theatre that evening, watching actors stumble cheerfully across the stage, the Knight found he was paying very little attention to the play.

He was paying attention to her instead.

The way she laughed at all the right moments and a few of the wrong ones. The way she leaned forward when something on stage surprised her. The way she turned to him with bright eyes after a particularly good scene, as if checking whether he'd caught it too — as if his reaction mattered, as if they were already the kind of people who shared things.

The Knight had read about love, of course. Every knight had. It featured heavily in the ballads — grand declarations on clifftops, heroic sacrifices, that sort of thing. He had always assumed, when it happened to him, that it would arrive with some ceremony. A thunderclap, perhaps. A meaningful glance across a crowded banquet hall. Something with a bit of drama to it.

He had not expected it to arrive in the middle of a theatre in Antwerpshire, on an ordinary Tuesday, after seven shops and a pair of silver trousers.

The play ended. The audience applauded. The Princess turned to him, still laughing at the final scene, and said something he would later be completely unable to recall — because at that precise moment, looking at her face in the warm glow of the theatre lights, the Knight understood something with total and slightly terrifying clarity.

He was in love.

Not the clifftop kind, or the ballad kind, or the grand-gesture kind. Something quieter and more certain than any of that. The kind that sneaks up on you between the third shop and the seventh, between a stuck knee guard and a shared laugh outside a closed door, and then sits down in your chest like it has always lived there and simply forgot to introduce itself until now.

He walked her through the lamplit streets of Antwerpshire to where her coach was waiting, and when they said goodnight he smiled and told her he'd had a lovely time — which was, without question, the single greatest understatement of his entire knightly career.

He stood there long after the coach had rounded the corner and disappeared into the night, the new trousers tucked under his arm, a ridiculous smile on his face, and the very clear and certain feeling that something had just begun.
