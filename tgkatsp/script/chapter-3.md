# Title: The Goofy Knight and the Silly Princess

# Chapter 3: The Curse of Ghenta the Grim

It began, as trouble often does, without much warning.

One morning the Princess was simply gone.

No note. No farewell. Just an empty room in the castle and a faint smell of something unpleasant — like damp stone and old ambition — that hadn't been there the night before. The King and Queen were beside themselves. The squires searched the grounds. The Knight, upon hearing the news, felt something cold settle in his chest and immediately began saddling his horse.

It did not take long to find out what had happened. Word travels fast in Antwerpshire, and the word was this: Ghenta the Grim had come.

Ghenta the Grim was a witch of considerable power and considerably poor temperament, who resided in the grey and drizzly city of Ghentmoor, two days' ride to the west. She was known for three things: her powerful staff, which crackled with dark magic at the slightest provocation; her devoted band of peasants, who followed her with the unthinking loyalty of people who had simply never thought to question anything; and her deep, abiding resentment of anyone who seemed to be having a good time.

The Princess, by all accounts, had been having a very good time indeed. Ghenta had taken exception to this.

And so the Princess had been taken to Ghentmoor, to the Old University — a vast, crumbling, thoroughly joyless building in the centre of the city — where she was now expected to serve Ghenta and her peasants indefinitely. Fetch this. Carry that. No, not like that. Do it again.

The Princess, it must be said, was not enjoying Ghentmoor.

The city was grey. The people were grey. Even the sky above it seemed to have given up and gone grey, as if the sun had taken one look at Ghentmoor and decided it had better things to do. The Old University was cold and smelled of cabbage and old grudges, and the peasants regarded her with the blank, slightly suspicious expressions of people who had never encountered anyone with a personality before and weren't entirely sure what to make of it.

She had been there for three days when she met François.

He appeared on the windowsill of the room where she was folding an enormous pile of Ghenta's laundry — which was, she felt, an indignity too far — and he regarded her with the expression of someone who has arrived somewhere and found it considerably beneath his standards.

He was a frog. Small, green, impeccably self-possessed. He looked at the laundry. He looked at the Princess. He looked back at the laundry.

"Mon dieu," he said.

"Hello," said the Princess.

"You are folding the laundry of zis woman?" He said this woman with the particular inflection of someone for whom two words were sufficient to convey an entire essay of contempt. "Zis is not acceptable. Zis is not acceptable at all."

"I'm aware," said the Princess.

François hopped from the windowsill to the top of the laundry pile and surveyed the room with the air of a general assessing a battlefield. "You 'ave someone? Someone who would come for you, if they knew where you were?"

The Princess thought of the Knight — of courtyard torches and theatre seats and the particular way he looked at her when he thought she wasn't paying attention.

"Yes," she said. "I have someone."

"Bien." François drew himself up to his full height, which was not considerable but was delivered with great conviction. "Tell me where to find zis person. I will carry ze message. I am, after all, an excellent messenger."

"Have you done this before?"

A pause.

"Ze details are not important," said François.

The Princess decided, on balance, that an overconfident French frog was still significantly better than no frog at all, and told him where to find the Knight. François listened with great seriousness, repeated the directions back incorrectly twice, corrected himself with considerable dignity, and then hopped back to the windowsill.

"Do not worry," he said, pausing dramatically in the frame. "François does not fail."

He disappeared into the grey Ghentmoor sky.

The Princess went back to folding the laundry, and allowed herself, for the first time in three days, a small smile.

---

The Knight was in the middle of a very bad week when François arrived on his windowsill.

He had spent three days making enquiries, sending scouts, and pacing the length of his chambers in a manner his squire found deeply exhausting. He had been about to ride to Ghentmoor regardless, without a plan, on the basis that doing something wrong felt better than doing nothing right.

Then the frog appeared.

"You are ze Knight?" said François, looking him up and down with an expression that suggested the Knight had not entirely met expectations.

"I am," said the Knight.

"She said you were taller," said François.

"She absolutely did not say that."

"Non," François conceded, with a small wave of one leg. "She did not. But you should work on your posture regardless. Now. Listen carefully. Ze Princess is held in ze Old University of Ghentmoor, in ze east wing, third floor. Ghenta ze Grim 'as 'er serving ze peasants. Ze staff is ze source of 'er power — destroy ze staff and ze witch is 'armless. Ze peasants, without ze magic to back zem up, are simply —" he paused, searching for the right word — "unpleasant. Which is manageable."

The Knight stared at him. "How do you know all of this?"

"I am an excellent observer," said François. "Also I 'id in ze witch's hat for approximately forty-five minutes zis morning. It was not comfortable but it was informative."

The Knight decided he liked this frog enormously.

He rode for Ghentmoor before the hour was out.

---

Ghentmoor was exactly as unpleasant as advertised. The Knight arrived at dusk, when the grey city was at its greiest, and made his way through the winding streets toward the Old University with his hood up and his hand on his sword and François riding in his breast pocket, occasionally offering directional commentary that was about seventy percent accurate.

The University loomed at the end of a cobbled street like a building that had decided long ago to be as unwelcoming as architecturally possible and had succeeded admirably. The Knight slipped in through a side entrance, dispatched two peasant guards with the minimum of fuss — they were, as François had predicted, more unpleasant than dangerous — and found the east wing staircase.

Third floor. End of the corridor. He could hear Ghenta before he could see her — a sharp, irritable voice issuing a long list of complaints about the quality of the evening's service.

He rounded the corner and there was the great hall of the east wing, lit by cold torches, full of grey-faced peasants — and there, in the middle of it all, was the Princess, carrying an enormous tray with the expression of someone mentally composing a very long list of grievances.

Their eyes met across the hall.

She looked, in order: relieved, delighted, and then immediately focused — because Ghenta the Grim had turned around.

The witch was tall and angular, with the kind of face that had settled permanently into dissatisfaction, and she held her staff in one hand like an extension of her own bad mood. It crackled with dark energy at the tip. Her eyes found the Knight and narrowed.

"A visitor," she said, with the tone of someone for whom visitors were just another thing to be annoyed about. "How tedious."

What followed was, by any measure, a significant commotion.

The Knight drew his sword. The peasants surged forward with the enthusiasm of people who had been waiting for something to happen. François, from the breast pocket, shouted instructions in French that were almost certainly unhelpful but were delivered with tremendous conviction. The Knight fought his way through the crowd — not elegantly, but effectively — and reached the Princess just as she had set down the tray with considerable purpose.

"You came," she said.

"Obviously," said the Knight.

There was no time for more than that, because Ghenta was raising her staff.

What the witch had not accounted for — what she could not have accounted for, because it had not yet been established as a pattern — was that the Princess, when the situation called for it, was considerably faster than she looked.

Before the staff could discharge so much as a spark, the Princess crossed the distance between them in four quick strides, grabbed the staff with both hands, and brought it down across her knee with a crack that echoed through the entire Old University.

The dark energy at the tip sputtered. Flickered. Went out.

Ghenta the Grim stared at the two broken halves of her staff. Then at the Princess. Then at the staff again.

"That," she said, in a very small voice, "was my staff."

"Yes," said the Princess pleasantly. "It was."

Without the magic crackling behind her, Ghenta the Grim was revealed to be exactly what she had always been underneath — a rather tired, rather cold, rather unhappy woman in a grey city who had run out of things to threaten people with. The peasants, sensing the shift, shuffled uncertainly and began, one by one, to find reasons to be somewhere else.

The Knight looked at the Princess. The Princess looked at the Knight. François, from the breast pocket, looked at both of them.

"Bien," said François, with great satisfaction. "Now can we please leave zis terrible city."

They rode out of Ghentmoor as the last of the evening light disappeared behind the grey horizon, and the Princess did not look back once. Not at the Old University, not at the cheerless streets, not at the city that had kept her for far too long.

She looked forward, toward the road home, toward Antwerpshire, toward the warm lit windows and the cobbled streets and everything that waited for her there.

Beside her, the Knight rode in silence for a while. Then, because he could not help it:

"You broke the staff over your knee."

"I did," said the Princess.

"Just like that."

"It wasn't that dramatic."

"It was extremely dramatic," said the Knight. "It was one of the most dramatic things I have ever seen."

The Princess considered this. "Good," she said, and smiled.

From the breast pocket, François made a sound of deep Parisian contentment.

They rode home through the dark, and the stars above Antwerpshire were very bright that night, and the Knight thought — not for the first time, and certainly not for the last — that he was the luckiest knight in the entire kingdom.
